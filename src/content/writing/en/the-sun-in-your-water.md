## Water Has A Memory

Before we go anywhere with the sun, you have to know about a Japanese researcher named Masaru Emoto (1943–2014). His work changed how I look at the glass on my desk.

Emoto did something simple and stubborn: he took water samples, exposed them to different inputs — written words, spoken words, music, prayers — then froze them and photographed the ice crystals under a microscope.

![Masaru Emoto — The Hidden Messages in Water](/writing/the-sun-in-your-water/the-sun-in-your-water-3.webp)  
*Masaru Emoto — The Hidden Messages in Water*

What he found:

→ Water exposed to "love," "gratitude," "thank you" formed beautiful, complete, hexagonal crystal structures — geometric, symmetric, alive-looking.

→ Water exposed to "hate," "fool," "you make me sick" formed broken, chaotic, ugly patterns — like the structure had been violated.

→ Mozart and Beethoven produced different patterns than heavy metal. Prayers produced different patterns than insults. The water *recorded* what was around it.

His most famous book, *The Hidden Messages in Water* (2004), became a global bestseller. The mainstream scientific community largely dismissed it — the methodology wasn't tight enough, the photography too selective. Fair criticism.

But here's the kicker:

Emoto's central claim — that water holds and transmits structure from its environment — turned out to be the soft poetic version of what hard science is now confirming in the lab.
