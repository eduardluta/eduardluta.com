Independence vs. Autonomy

5 min read

There's a subtle but crucial difference between independence and autonomy that most people miss. Understanding this distinction has fundamentally changed how I approach building companies and living life.

The Independence Trap

Independence is about being free from something. It's reactive. When you seek independence, you're defining yourself by what you're escaping‚Äîwhether that's a job, a relationship, or a system you don't like.

I see this all the time in entrepreneurship. People start businesses because they hate having a boss. They want to be "independent." But then they end up with fifty bosses‚Äîtheir clients‚Äîand they're more trapped than before.

Independence is freedom from constraints. Autonomy is freedom to choose your constraints.

What Autonomy Actually Means

Autonomy is different. It's about being free to do something. It's proactive. It means you have the agency to make meaningful choices about your life and work, even within constraints.

Here's what I've learned: you can have autonomy without independence. A developer at a great company might have tremendous autonomy over their work‚Äîchoosing their projects, setting their schedule, deciding how to solve problems‚Äîeven though they're not "independent" in the traditional sense.

The Three Components of Autonomy

  • Control over outcomes - You decide what success looks like
  • Control over process - You choose how to achieve those outcomes
  • Control over timing - You manage when things happen

Why This Matters for Builders

When building products or companies, chasing independence often leads to isolation. You reject help, avoid partnerships, and insist on doing everything yourself because you're trying to be "free from" dependencies.

But seeking autonomy? That leads somewhere different. You're willing to work with others, take investment, or join existing systems‚Äîas long as you maintain meaningful control over the important decisions.

At dua.com, we have investors and advisors. We're not "independent" in that sense. But we have complete autonomy over our product vision, our culture, and how we serve our users. That autonomy has been more valuable than any illusion of independence.

The Practical Shift

Stop asking: "How can I be free from this?"

Start asking: "How can I gain more agency within this?"

The second question leads to better answers. It lets you build on existing systems instead of reinventing everything. It allows for collaboration and leverage. It's how you actually move fast and build things that matter.

Independence is overrated. Autonomy is what you really want.
